The story

Months before COVID-19 brought the US to a standstill, school administrators at the Jesuit founded, Carroll College in Helena MT unceremoniously canceled a planned drag show event being held by the campus' Gay Straight Aliance (GSA). The show, which had originally been approved by campus admin, had recruited an unprecendented number of performers, and promised to be a celebration of the LGBTQIA+ community in Helena.

Students may have seen this coming. There had been a number of complaints that campus ministry--a powerful institution at a small Catholic college--was espousing messages that contained covert incitement to impinge on the GSA's rights to expression. And that ultimately those messages were empowering anti-LGBTQIA+ expression in the wider Carroll College and Helena community. It did not help that insiders in the administration had reported that campus executives were highly influenced by one Father Marc Lenneman on campus, who had strong connections to external donors, and a known (if heavily implied) aversion to the GSA's activities on campus.

Helena, and Montana broadly, is home a diverse number of views amongst the populace. But the vast majority of folks have a shared understanding that white supremacist speech is problematic. If you find yourself in Helena, a lot of local life revolves around going to the cafes down Last Chance Gulch and heading into the downtown area. At least during the day. At night it's mostly the same, but centered on the large number of breweries in the capital city. Sit down and talk to anyone from Helena and you'll find out how litle patience they have with white supremacy. White supremacists cause trouble without contributing to society, and contributing to your home is a cornerstone of Montanans' cultural understanding of themselves. Even so, The Hill reported in 2022 that Montana housed the highest number of white supremacist hate groups in the nation.

You might be asking yourself, "what the hell? How did this go from talking about anti-LGBTQIA+ sentiment to discussing white supremacy in the state??" And the answer is . . . well it's mostly just plain upsetting. White supremacist groups have historically been strong supporters of what's been called the "birther movement" in the United States. No, not the one where Trump wanted to see Obama's birth certificate. The one where they want to force more women to have more babies. It's a response to a core tenent/anxiety at the heart of the "Great Replacement Theory". You might see where this is going. This part of the movement has strong ties to eugenicist ideologies, and has often been co-opted by white supremacists groups in an effort to increase the number of white Americans to offset the perceived increase in both immigration and non-white births. LGBTQIA+ sentiment runs deep in these subsets of the white supremacist movement, if for no other reason than LGBTQIA+ folks are not "contributing" to the "goals" of white supremacists who are plugged in to replacement ideology. This makes LGBTQIA+ groups, and individuals, targets for white supremacist hate.

What we wanted to know with this report was how much could Fr. Marc's rhetoric be predicated on sentiments expressed by white supremacist, ant-LGBTQIA+ ideology. We did not assume that Fr. Marc would make overt statements that could be traced directly to the ideology. But we did want to know whether the ideas expressed in his public speeches might be similar to those found in white-supremacist circles. We wanted to trace covert references to such ideologies. And we wanted to do it quantitatively--because while qualitative evidence is just as important and necessary as quantitative evidence, quantitiative studies give use the ability to directly quantify how confident we are in our conclusions.

I also just happen to like quantitative methods. I think they're fun. Come at me.

This work was originaly initiated in late 2021. At the time, Fr. Marc was running a podcast online with a series of homilies that he had given at Carroll College. That became our source material. Because the controversy surrounding the drag show happened around February 2020, we collected the recordings of those homilies that were given at the end of January, and through the middle of March. In revisiting this project, we wanted to collect additional homily transcriptions from before and after the dates we had in our data, however that podcast appears to no longer be indexed on google and may no longer exist online.


Giving grace and the opportunity to learn from past mistakes.

I'd like to stress right here right now that these analyses may not reflect where Fr. Marc sits in his opinions with respect to LGBTQIA+ folks now. Time is an interesting thing, and human minds are mutable. And so while this analysis indeed will look at the ways that anti-LGBTQIA+ sentiment was expressed in the weeks leading up to, and following Carroll College's decision to cancel the GSA's drag show event, it does not mean that Fr. People grow and change. I'd like to think that we can give this person the opportunity to do the same.

Methods

Message Content Analysis:
We first generated (1) 20 iterations of various hate speech related ideas relating to antisemitic sentiment using a large language model. These examples were (2) used as proxies to represent the way a person might coherently express an antisemitic idea online. We then (3) created a custom semantic search index for all comments made to a single reddit conversation/comments made to a single submission.

One important note here is that we were specifically looking for potential covert hate speech in this project. That is to say, we were looking for instances in which hate-adjacent sentiment is expressed indirectly or implied. From a natural language processing (NLP) perspective, this can be (and is here) measured in how much semantic similarity there is between one written message and another. If there is high similarity between the two, it means one of two things: either (1) what's being said in the two compared messages is the same or (2) you can dang well predict what's being said in one message upon hearing the other one. For covert hate speech, this is the same as saying "the author here didn't say something hateful directly, but what they said points heavily and quantitatively in the direction of a hate based message."

The examples provided and reported on in this report were identified using our semantic search function and have not been altered or summarized in any way using a large language model. All data, thus, is human in origin and not "hallucinated".

Influence/Convergence Analysis:
Rosen and Dale 2023 proposed an unsupervised approach for analyzing linguistic convergence in datasets. Their definition is such that when you can recover more of the information about an earlier text from reading a second text, there is evidence of "convergence"--of the later text borrowing ideas from the earlier text. This can be used to assess the profundity of influence that prior texts have on subsequent texts. This analysis forms the statistical backbone of our influence analytics reporting in the last section, and provides direct insight into how influential the ideas in comments are based on whether those same ideas propagate in later comments.

Message Influence Analysis

When a particular message is introduced it can have multiple effects. One of those effects is to trigger convergence in subsequent comments. Convergence is the tendency for individuals to adopt similar ways of expressing themselves and their ideas. But it can also occur intrapersonally, where, if a speaker receives enough positive reinforcement for a given way of expressing an idea, they might repeat it in subsequent examples too.

We took the example comments shown in our previous, sunflower plot and used those as starting points to analyze what the effect was on subsequent expressions across the entirety of the corpus. The result is a visualization showing how previous ways of expressing anti-LGBTQIA+ sentiment influence subsequent expressions over time.

The thicker the line between two dates in the sankey (or flow) plot below, the more that expressions of anti-LGBTQIA+ sentiment from the previous date (on the left) influenced any of the content expressed in subsequent speeches (on the right). You can explore and see which dates exhibited greater influence by hovering over the connections between dates to see the connection strength between the two of them. The connection strength is measured in the number of total additional bits of information that are shared between the anti-LGBTQIA+ sentiment expressing lines from the previous date and the lines in the subsequent date.
